The first layer: the foundation everything else is built on

The first layer is the layer of glue every other layer stands on. Get the nozzle-to-bed gap and the speed right and most beginner print failures simply vanish. Watch it, in side-view cross-section.

What you just watched

A first layer sticks or fails on two things. First, the nozzle-to-bed gap: too far and the plastic lands as round, un-pressed lines that barely touch — gaps open up and a corner curls off the bed mid-print; too close and it's crushed into torn, starved ridges with an elephant's foot bulge; just right is a slight squish that flattens each line and welds neighbours into one solid sheet. Second, the speed: a fast first layer drags a thin, weakly-bonded line, while a slow first layer gives the plastic time to press down and grip. Nail those two and roughly nine out of ten beginner print failures disappear — because every other layer is standing on this one.

Honest note — this is stylized. This is an exaggerated side view: the real squish on a good first layer is a fraction of a millimetre, and the colours and scale here are pushed way up so you can see what's going on. Every printer's "just right" gap is a little different — the picture on your bed is the one that matters, so trust what you see there over any drawing, including this one.
