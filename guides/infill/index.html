Infill — what's inside your print, and how much you need

Almost every print is hollow, filled with a light lattice. Watch what the percentage really buys you — and why one more wall beats cranking it up. About 25 seconds.

What you just watched

A print is mostly air. Inside the solid walls sits a light lattice — the infill — and the percentage just sets how dense that lattice is. Turning it up adds plastic and time in a roughly straight line, but adds surprisingly little real-world strength.

The strength lives in the walls and the solid top and bottom. That's why one more wall beats 20 % more infill — it's stiffer for less plastic and less time. Infill's real job is quieter: holding the flat top up so it doesn't sag, and resisting a squeeze. So keep infill low (15–20 %), and when a part must be strong, add a wall instead of cranking the dial.

Honest note — this is stylized. The lattices, the meters, and the sag are drawn big and clean to make the point visible. Real infill is finer, the strength gap depends on your exact part, load, and filament, and the "walls beat infill" rule has limits (a part crushed flat wants more infill too). The only honest number for your part is the one your own slicer's time-and-filament estimate shows you — trust the real print over any drawing, this one included.
