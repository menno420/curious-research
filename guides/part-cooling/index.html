Part-cooling — the breeze that freezes your prints

Fresh-laid plastic is hot and soft. A little fan freezes it before gravity can drag it down. Watch it in about 20 seconds.

What you just watched

Plastic leaves the nozzle hot and soft. Left alone, gravity drags it down before it hardens — so overhangs droop, bridges sag, and tiny tips melt into blobs. The part-cooling fan blows a breeze that freezes each fresh line in place before it can move. That's why more fan = sharper shapes.

But cooling has a price: a line that froze fast didn't get as long to fuse into the line below it. For PLA that's fine — it fuses easily, so it can run the fan wide open. For PETG (and ABS), too much fan means weak, splitting layers, so it wants far less — just enough for overhangs. Same knob, opposite settings.

Honest note — this is stylized. The droop, the blobs, and the colours are exaggerated to make the point visible. Real cooling effects are smaller and depend on your exact printer, part, and speed. There's no substitute for printing the little test and looking at the underside yourself — trust the real plastic over any drawing, including this one.
