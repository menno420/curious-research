The arm's safe envelope — and why a software clamp matters

A servo is a motor you tell to go to an exact angle. Your arm has six of them; here we watch just the shoulder joint, and watch what happens when a command aims somewhere the joint should never go.

What you just watched

1. Each joint is turned by one servo — a motor you command to a specific angle. A real arm has six; we showed one.

2. The green wedge is that joint's envelope: every angle it can reach without hitting itself, the desk, or a wire. You measure its real min and max once.

3. A command past the max (170°) with nothing checking it drives the joint straight into the desk. That is how gears strip and brackets crack.

4. A clamp — clamp(angle, min, max) — compares every command to the measured min/max and trims anything out of range down to the edge, before the motor moves. The wrong number never reaches the servo.

Safety, for real

  • The clamp is only software. It works only if the min/max were measured correctly and every motion routine actually routes through it. No motion code merges without the clamp in the path.
  • Servo power is external, always — a separate 5–6 V supply with a shared ground, fused, with a reachable power switch. Never the Arduino's 5 V pin.
  • A human watches every move. The arm moves only inside its calibrated envelope, and only with you standing there, hand near the switch.

What this animation simplifies

It shows angles only. A real joint also fights gravity, momentum, and wire snag; a real arm has six servos; and the true envelope is a 3-D volume, not a flat wedge. Measure first, clamp always, watch every time.
