How 3D-print clearance works: why parts jam, and how a little gap fixes it

Two printed parts the exact same size will weld together instead of fitting. The trick is leaving a deliberate air gap — but it counts per side, and the very bottom lies to you. Watch all four in about 20 seconds.

What you just watched

1. Zero gap fails. Two printed parts made the exact same size don't fit — the melted plastic squishes out and they weld into one blob. Clearance is the deliberate air gap you leave between two parts so they can actually fit.

2. Clearance counts per side. The gap sits on both walls, so it adds up across the whole width: 0.20 mm each side becomes 0.40 mm across the diameter. If you want 0.40 mm of total play, you design in 0.20 mm per side. Forgetting the doubling is the classic first-print mistake.

3. The fit ladder. As the gap grows you climb: press fit (tight, holds by itself, needs a squeeze) → snug / push fit (slides in by hand, no wobble) → sliding fit (moves freely, tiny play) → loose fit (drops in, rattles). Pick the rung the job needs.

4. Elephant's foot skews the bottom. The nozzle presses the first layer down into the hot bed, so it spreads wider than the rest. That makes the bottom of a hole tighter than the top — the part lies about its fit. Fix: turn on elephant foot compensation (about 0.2 mm) in your slicer.

Honest simplification note: real fit also depends on your filament, nozzle temperature, print speed, and part shape — this shows the idea. To get the exact numbers for your printer, print the companion project: the tolerance-test coin, which measures these gaps on your own machine.
