Why a lithophane glows — thickness makes the picture

A lithophane is a thin plastic panel whose thickness changes across a picture. Put a light behind it and the photo appears — made of shadow. Watch how it works, then why you print it standing upright.

What you just watched

1. A lithophane is a picture stored as thickness. Thick plastic blocks the light (dark); thin plastic lets it through (bright). Thickness in, brightness out.

2. Because only thickness varies, a plain lithophane is grayscale by nature — light and dark, not color. (True color needs the HueForge filament-stacking trick — see the guide's "full-color stretch" section, and the honest costs that come with it.)

3. Print it upright. A printer's side-to-side detail is far finer than its layer-to-layer detail, so standing the panel up turns the tones smooth instead of stair-stepped.

Simplification note: a real lithophane has thousands of thickness values across the picture, not the 9 columns shown here, and the brightness shown is illustrative — a teaching cartoon of how light falls off through plastic, not a physics-exact curve.
