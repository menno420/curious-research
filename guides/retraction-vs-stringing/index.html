Retraction vs stringing: why prints grow hairs — and how to stop it

One slider in your slicer decides whether the gaps between parts come out bare or covered in fine plastic hairs. Watch the hot end cross an open gap six times and see exactly what that slider is doing inside the nozzle.

What you just watched

1. While the nozzle prints a wall, the motor keeps pushing filament in like a piston, so the little melt pocket above the hole builds up pressure — primed to flow.

2. Cross a gap with that pressure still loaded and nothing pulling back, and plastic oozes out the whole way — the nozzle drags it into a thin string.

3. Retraction briefly pulls the filament backward first, which releases the pocket's pressure and stops the ooze at the tip.

4. With pressure released the nozzle crosses clean, then primes (pushes back in) on arrival — a tiny tug for direct drive, a bigger one for Bowden.

5. Pull back too far and you trade stringing for a jam (heat creep), gaps, blobs, or ground filament — more is not better.

6. Print a stringing tower that sweeps the value up its height, then read it top-to-bottom and pick the setting where the strings just disappear.

Simplification note: real melt flow is messier than this cartoon — the plastic gets runnier the faster it flows and the hotter it is (it isn't one fixed "liquid"), the hot-to-cold boundary inside the hot end is a fuzzy gradient not a hard line, and the nozzle's exact shape matters. The biggest hidden gotcha isn't a setting at all: if your filament is damp, absorbed water flashes to steam and makes it string and sputter no matter how perfect your retraction. Dry the filament first, then trust the tower.

Stay safe: the hot end runs 200–250 °C and will burn you; the motors and heaters carry real power. Claude can explain and help you set up the slicer, but you power the printer, you slice, and you start and watch every print yourself — never leave one running on the strength of a guide.
