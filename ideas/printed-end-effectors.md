# Printed end-effectors — a swappable tool family built around one standard mount

> **State:** grown via the ritual (2026-07-13) → verdict: **build** — but build the *mount standard first*, then a single-servo gripper as the family's second member (the [pen holder](../projects/arm-pen-plotter/) is already the first). The whole family prints and hand-fits with **zero arm motion and zero power** — the printer supplies the accuracy the servos can't.

The 6-servo arm gains new jobs by wearing printed tools — a 2-finger gripper, a magnet pickup, a scoop, a phone clamp, a suction cup — and the real idea isn't any single tool: it's a **shared wrist mount** so every tool swaps onto the same interface. The arm upgrades itself on the printers, and because you're "just printing brackets," you sidestep every servo-precision headache — the *printer* provides the millimetre accuracy the arm lacks. (Printer↔arm crossover; the dossier's [Part 3 #1, "DO THIS FIRST"](../research/possibility-dossier.md).)

*(Bench terms, one clause each: **end-effector / EOAT** = whatever's bolted to the arm's last joint — the tool that actually touches the world (gripper, pen, suction cup); **payload** = the weight the arm can hold at the tool without sagging or stalling; **stall torque** = the twist a servo makes at the instant it's fully jammed — a hold-for-a-few-seconds number, not a working one; **usable / continuous torque** = the honest ~50 % of stall you design around so the servo doesn't cook; **derating** = deliberately planning for that lower usable number; **servo horn** = the little splined arm that clamps onto the servo shaft — where a tool bolts on; **rack-and-pinion** = a round gear (pinion) driving two straight toothed bars (racks) in opposite directions, turning one servo's spin into two jaws closing; **compliant / Fin Ray** = a springy finger that bends to wrap around an object instead of gripping it rigidly; **flexure / living hinge** = a thin printed section that bends on purpose in place of a pin joint; **TPU** = a rubbery, stretchy filament for those bendy parts; **PETG** = a tougher-than-PLA rigid filament that bends before it snaps; **tool changer / quick-change** = a standard coupling so tools swap fast onto one interface; **kinematic coupling** = three balls seating into three grooves so a removed tool returns to the exact same pose every time; **clamp / envelope** = the safe joint-angle range you measured, plus the software that trims every command back inside it before it reaches a motor.)*

## The 8 questions

**1. What is this thing, really?**
It's a set of printed tools the arm wears at its wrist — its *end-effector* is simply whatever is bolted to the last joint (gripper, pen, suction cup), the part that touches the world. The honest core insight the ritual surfaced: the value isn't *collecting* effectors, it's **standardizing the mount** so they interchange. A tool bolts to the servo horn, and — the critical detail — that joint must be *bolted* (M2/M3), never glued or press-fit alone, or its slop adds straight onto the arm's own error ([ThinkRobotics: printed gripper design](https://thinkrobotics.com/blogs/learn/designing-a-3d-printed-gripper-for-a-robotic-arm)). The [pen holder already shipped](../projects/arm-pen-plotter/) inside the pen-plotter kit is the family's *first* member — its `arm_mount()` module (a 2-screw servo-horn plate, `mount_mode="horn"`) is already the seed of the shared interface. This idea is: lift that seed into a standalone standard every future tool inherits.

**2. What could it grow into?**
A quick-change tool library: gripper, magnet pickup, scoop, hook, phone clamp, suction cup, pen — each printed once, all snapping onto one plate. In industry a quick-change coupler connects the tool in a single motion and its whole point is cutting tool-change time and boosting versatility ([eoat.net quick-change](https://eoat.net/components/quick-change-system/), [OnRobot Quick Changer](https://onrobot.com/en/products/quick-changer)). The fun ceiling is a **magnetic + kinematic** quick-change: magnets hold, three balls-in-grooves locate, and a tool snaps back to a repeatable pose in seconds — a commercial hobby-scale version (RoboMag) uses 3D-printed ultralight parts and swaps in under 3 s ([Magnetics.com RoboMag](https://www.magnetics.com/products/robomag-quick-changer)). The 3-color printer can color-code the whole family so each tool is obvious at a glance.

**3. What's the coolest version of the simplest build?**
Not a fancy gripper first — the **standard mount plate plus one passive tool you test entirely by hand**. Concretely: a printed magnet-pickup (a holder for a permanent magnet) bolted to the shared plate, hand-fitted to the arm's wrist horn with the **power off and no motion at all**. That's the coolest simplest honest win because it (a) proves the interface, (b) costs *zero* servo channels — the arm already spends all six on its joints — and (c) is immediately useful: the arm can lift a steel part. Passive tools like a magnet pickup, hook, scoop, or the pen holder are the standard low-risk 6-axis effectors ([VEX: 6-axis end effectors](https://kb.vex.com/hc/en-us/articles/27460770024980-Understanding-the-6-Axis-Robotic-Arm-End-Effectors); industrial magnetic grabheads: [Magswitch](https://magswitch.com/products/d120)). A single-servo gripper is the natural *second* active member, once the plate is proven.

**4. What breaks it? (be honest — torque, payload, print strength)**
Four honest breakers:
- **Payload is tiny, and the tool's own weight eats it.** The wrist servos are the small ones (SG90 / MG90S class, ~1.8–2.2 kg·cm stall) sitting at the end of the longest lever, so realistic usable payload at the gripper is **tens to low-hundreds of grams — call ~50–200 g an optimistic ceiling** including the object, derived from the joint-torque math and the derate rule ([torque sizing](https://automaticaddison.com/how-to-determine-what-torque-you-need-for-your-servo-motors/), [RobotShop torque calculator](https://community.robotshop.com/tutorials/show/robot-arm-torque-calculator)). A printed effector itself weighs ~30–120 g, and that mass hangs at the very end of the lever, so it comes straight off the budget. Design around **~50 % of stall torque**, not the box number — stall is a hold-a-few-seconds-then-overheat figure ([servo torque curves](https://www.motioncontroltips.com/servo-motor-torque-curve/)).
- **A gripper *deliberately* stalls against the object, and SG90 plastic gears strip when stalled.** Real specs: **SG90** ~1.8 kg·cm / 9 g (plastic gears), **MG90S** ~2.2 kg·cm / 13.4 g (metal), **MG996R** ~9.4–11 kg·cm / 55 g ([SG90 vs MG90S](https://zbotic.in/mg90s-vs-sg90-torque-accuracy-best-uses-compared/), [MG996R datasheet](https://components101.com/motors/mg996r-servo-motor-datasheet)). For anything that clamps, MG90S or MG996R is the honest choice over SG90 — but MG996R adds 55 g of end-of-arm mass and pulls ~2.5 A at 6 V stall, which is exactly why servo power must be a separate fused supply, never the Arduino 5 V pin ([MG996R datasheet](https://components101.com/motors/mg996r-servo-motor-datasheet)).
- **Print strength has a weak axis.** FDM parts break *between* layers — inter-layer bond is the weakest feature — so a load-bearing tool must be oriented so its main load runs *along* the layers (in-plane), never peeling them apart ([layer-adhesion strength study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9230522/), [PLA/PETG strength guide](https://www.3dsourced.com/guides/how-strong-are-3d-printed-parts-pla-abs-petg/)). PLA is stiff but **brittle — it snaps with no warning**; PETG bends/whitens first and has better layer adhesion, so it's the honest pick for frames, jaws, and mount plates ([PETG vs PLA](https://ultimaker.com/learn/petg-vs-pla-vs-abs-3d-printing-strength-comparison/)).
- **Suction is oversold on a hobby arm.** A vacuum gripper needs a pump or venturi + a solenoid valve to release + tubing + a relay ([vacuum-gripper build](https://automaticaddison.com/robotic-arm-with-vacuum-suction-cup-for-pick-and-place/), [low-voltage arm-vacuum project](https://github.com/maker101io/roboticArmVacuum)). A small 3.7–6 V diaphragm pump (~65 g) can lift **light, flat, non-porous** objects only; porous/textured/heavy items leak and drop, and the pump+valve+tubing mass eats the §1 budget. Sizing vacuum is genuinely non-linear and hard ([Vention: vacuum-gripper sizing](https://vention.io/blogs/getting-started-with-vacuum-grippers-431)). Honest verdict on suction: a light pick-and-place novelty, not a route to heavier payload — the mechanical gripper does more per gram.

**5. What does it let me build next?**
It compounds harder than any other crossover — the dossier ranks it **#1** for exactly this reason. Once the mount standard exists, every future arm job just *prints a new tool*: pick-and-place, teach-mode replays, the [stepped-timelapse phone clamp](arm-camera-timelapse.md) (dossier #3, "the phone clamp is a printed end-effector"), the [pen plotter](arm-pen-plotter.md). The standard **is** the multiplier — without it, every new tool is a from-scratch wrist redesign; with it, they're all drop-ins. It also unlocks the session idea in this ritual's card: a passive bench **tool-dock** so the arm swaps tools hands-free via a taught sequence.

**6. What does it need?**
- **Parts:** filament — **PETG** for frames / jaws / the mount plate, and **TPU (~95A Shore)** for compliant fingertips, grippy pads, or Fin Ray fingers if the 3-color printer runs it (TPU stretches without cracking — the right material for anything that flexes; [TPU guide](https://mandarin3d.com/blog/tpu-flexible-filament-perfect-for-gaskets-grips-and-more)). **M2/M3 bolts** for the horn mount. A **permanent magnet** for the pickup tool. One **MG90S or MG996R** for the gripper's single servo (not SG90 — it strips on clamp-stall). Optionally a small **diaphragm pump + solenoid valve** for the suction experiment.
- **Print files:** parametric OpenSCAD, shipped as **`.scad` source only** — OpenSCAD is a verified wall in the build container (no renderer/slicer present), so you render locally (F6 → Export STL → slice on your machine), the same rule the [pen holder](../projects/arm-pen-plotter/) already follows (see `docs/CAPABILITIES.md`). Design clearance holes ~0.2 mm oversize (PLA/PETG shrink); expect one test-fit iteration.
- **Designs worth copying by name:** the [PapaBravo rack-and-pinion gripper](https://www.printables.com/model/403482-rack-pinion-robotic-gripper-jaw) (the canonical single-servo parallel jaw, sized around an MG996R) and single-servo [TPU Fin Ray fingers](https://makerworld.com/en/models/644386-fin-ray-gripper-ftc-single-servo) for a compliant grip. Mechanism trade-off: a **pivot/scissor** jaw is simplest (fewest parts), a **rack-and-pinion** parallel jaw grips flat objects better ([gripper mechanisms compared](https://zbotic.in/gripper-mechanism-parallel-angular-jaw-actuators/)).
- **Power (non-negotiable):** any tool with a servo runs off a **separate, fused 5–6 V supply with shared ground and a reachable switch**, sized for stall current — **never the Arduino's 5 V pin** (see the [arm-envelope guide's safety rules](../guides/arm-envelope-explained/guide.md)).

**7. Who does what — me, Claude, or both?**
- **You:** slice and print each tool, **bolt** it to the servo horn by hand (M2/M3 — never glue-only), test-fit on the arm's wrist with the **power off and no motion**, and — for anything that clamps or lifts — ⚠️ **verify it holds before trusting it** (a printed jaw or magnet failing mid-lift drops the object; that's a "check this yourself" step). Wire the external fused supply for any servo tool.
- **Claude:** design the parametric **standard mount plate** plus each effector as small, reviewable `.scad`, with the servo-horn bolt pattern as a *shared parameter* so every tool inherits one interface; write any gripper-servo control routed through `clamp()` against `arm/calibration.json` — no motion code merges without the clamp in the path.
- **Both:** dial in the test-fit tolerances (hole oversize, horn spline fit) against the first printed sample, and decide honestly which tools are worth their end-of-arm mass.

**8. What's the smallest piece I could finish this weekend?**
Design and print the **standard mount plate** — lifting the pen holder's `arm_mount()` into a standalone reusable interface — plus **one passive magnet-pickup tool**, and hand-fit both to the arm's wrist horn with the **power off**. That's a finished, real, zero-risk result by Sunday: the arm can pick up a steel part, and the interface every future tool inherits now exists. The single-servo rack-and-pinion gripper is next weekend; the suction and Fin Ray experiments are later curiosities.

## Verdict

**build.** But build the *interface*, not a pile of one-off tools.

The dossier already rates printed end-effectors the #1 printer↔arm crossover — highest value, lowest risk, most compounding — precisely because you sidestep servo precision (the *printer* provides the accuracy the arm can't) and every tool prints and hand-fits with **zero motion and zero power**. The ritual's sharpening: the one-liner listed "fingers / suction cup / pen holder" as if the goal were *collecting* effectors. The research says the goal is the **shared mount** — without it, every new tool is a from-scratch wrist redesign; with it, gripper, magnet pickup, scoop, phone clamp, and pen all drop onto one plate. The [pen holder](../projects/arm-pen-plotter/) is already family member #1, and its `arm_mount()` module is the seed to standardize.

Honest limits kept in view, not buried: usable payload is only **~50–200 g** and the printed tool's own **30–120 g** mass eats into it; a single-servo gripper needs **MG90S or MG996R** (an SG90 strips its plastic gears when it stalls against the object); **suction is a light-flat-non-porous novelty**, not a payload upgrade; print **frames in PETG, flexures in TPU**, and orient every load-bearing part so the force runs *along* the layers, not across them.

**First steps Claude will set up (interface first, no motion):**
1. **The standard mount plate** — a parametric `.scad` that lifts the pen holder's `arm_mount()` into a standalone reusable interface: the servo-horn bolt pattern + a keying feature, as shared parameters every effector imports. `.scad` source only (render/slice locally per `docs/CAPABILITIES.md`).
2. **A passive magnet-pickup tool** on that plate — zero servo channels, hand-fitted power-off, immediately useful for steel parts. ⚠️ *Check the magnet holds your part before trusting a lift.*
3. **A single-servo rack-and-pinion 2-finger gripper** (`.scad`, [PapaBravo](https://www.printables.com/model/403482-rack-pinion-robotic-gripper-jaw)-style) driven by one MG90S/MG996R — its control routed through `clamp()` against `arm/calibration.json`, on the external fused supply, watched every powered move. ⚠️ *Load-bearing: check the jaws hold.*
4. **(Optional, later)** a **TPU Fin Ray** compliant finger set for delicate objects, and a **light suction** experiment (small diaphragm pump + solenoid) — honestly scoped to flat, non-porous, lightweight items only.

---

**Safety — check this yourself:** every effector experiment obeys the arm rails. The tools themselves print and hand-fit with **no power and no motion** (that's the whole low-risk appeal), but the moment any tool moves under power: motion is **clamped to `arm/calibration.json`** (your measured envelope — the owner's step-1 measurement is still pending, so no motion runs until it exists), a **human watches with a hand on the switch**, and servo power is the **external fused 5–6 V supply with shared ground, never the Arduino 5 V pin**. Anything that **clamps or lifts is load-bearing** — ⚠️ verify the printed part actually holds your object before trusting it, since a jaw or magnet letting go mid-lift drops the load (per repo `CLAUDE.md` §2).
