# A printed diffuser + mic + LED strip desk lamp that pulses to sound — "reacts to loudness" is the honest weekend build; a per-frequency light show is the finicky stretch

> **State:** `grown` — full ritual, 2026-07-13. **Verdict: build** (the amplitude-reactive version — a microphone driving the brightness of a short LED ring inside a printed diffuser, all on USB power). The per-frequency FFT light show is the labeled stretch. See the end.

Original one-liner: *"A printed shade + Arduino mic + LED ring desk lamp that reacts to sound — the pure-Arduino starter."*

Grown honestly, the one-liner hides one big fork: **"reacts to sound" can mean two very different builds.** The easy, reliable one is *reacts to how loud the room is* — the lamp brightens and pulses with volume. The hard one is *reacts to which notes are playing* — bass lights the bottom red, treble the top blue. The first is a genuine weekend win on a plain Arduino; the second is real but finicky on this class of board, and Q4 is where that honesty lives.

*(Bench terms, one clause each: **electret mic amplifier** = a tiny microphone with a small amp chip on the same board, so it outputs a clean audio *waveform voltage* an Arduino can read — the [MAX4466](https://www.adafruit.com/product/1063) and [MAX9814](https://www.adafruit.com/product/1713) are the two common ones; **ADC** = the Arduino's analog-to-digital converter, the `analogRead()` that turns that voltage into a number; **amplitude / envelope** = how loud the sound is right now — the easy thing to measure; **FFT (Fast Fourier Transform)** = the math that splits a sound into its separate frequencies (bass vs treble) — the hard thing; **WS2812B / NeoPixel** = an addressable LED where each light has a tiny chip so one Arduino wire controls hundreds of them individually, sold as rings and strips; **level shifter** = a small chip that lifts a 3.3 V data signal up to the 5 V these LEDs expect — needed only on 3.3 V boards; **vase mode** = a slicer setting that prints an object as one continuous single-wall spiral with no seam, the go-to for thin translucent shades; **Tg / glass transition** = the temperature where a plastic goes soft — PLA's is low, which matters for anything warm.)*

## 1 · What is this thing, really?

A desk lamp whose brightness follows the sound in the room: a microphone board feeds an Arduino, the Arduino measures how loud it is, and it drives a ring or short strip of addressable LEDs behind a **3D-printed translucent shade** so the light pulses with the music. Honestly stated: the microphone-to-brightness loop is a well-trodden beginner Arduino build; the printed diffuser is where *your* printer earns its place in the project, turning a raw ring of LEDs into a soft, even glow.

## 2 · What could it grow into? (the fun ceiling)

Start with "the whole lamp pulses with the beat." Climb from there: a **VU-meter column** where louder = more LEDs lit; a slow **ambient mode** that drifts colors when the room is quiet and snaps to the beat when music plays; and the real ceiling — a **per-frequency spectrum lamp** where bass, mids, and treble each drive their own color/zone (Q4 is honest about how hard that last one is on a plain Arduino). The printed-shade half compounds too: once you can print a clean diffuser, the same skill makes lithophane night-lights, LED sign backers, and softbox panels — this idea is the sibling of [`lithophane-night-light.md`](lithophane-night-light.md), the repo's other "printed translucent thing + light" build.

## 3 · What's the coolest version of the simplest build?

**A short LED ring in a printed diffuser that brightens and pulses with the room's volume — powered entirely from one USB port.** A 16-LED ring, a MAX9814 microphone board, an Arduino Nano, and a vase-mode printed shade. The Arduino samples the mic over a short window, measures the loudness, and maps it to brightness (and a color that shifts with level). No FFT, no external power supply, no soldering marathon — a glowing object that *obviously reacts* to your music, finished in an evening. The printed shade is what makes it look like a lamp instead of a breadboard.

## 4 · What breaks it? (the honest realities)

**The microphone you pick decides whether it works at all.** The cheap **KY-038 sound sensor** (the one bundled in most starter kits) is built around an LM393 comparator and a potentiometer: its **digital pin is just a threshold — "is it louder than this knob setting? yes/no"**, a binary trigger, not a loudness reading ([microcontrollerslab KY-038](https://microcontrollerslab.com/ky-038-microphone-sound-sensor-module-arduino-tutorial/), [circuitdigest KY-038](https://circuitdigest.com/microcontroller-projects/interface-ky038-sound-sensor-with-esp32)). It *does* have an analog pin, but that output is commonly reported as **weak, unstable, and inverted** (louder reads as a *lower* voltage) with no proper mic-amp stage ([joy-it KY-038](https://sensorkit.joy-it.net/en/sensors/ky-038)). For music-reactive work you want an **electret amplifier board** instead: the **[MAX4466](https://www.adafruit.com/product/1063)** gives a clean audio waveform with adjustable gain (25×–125×), rail-to-rail up to ~5 Vpp — Adafruit calls it *"ideal for reading from a microcontroller ADC"* — and the **[MAX9814](https://www.adafruit.com/product/1713)** adds **automatic gain control**, which *"quiets nearby loud sounds so they don't clip and amplifies quiet far-away sounds"* ([Adafruit MAX9814](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-agc-electret-microphone-amplifier-max9814/overview)). For a lamp that meets music at changing volume, the **MAX9814 (AGC) is the honest pick**; the MAX4466 is great if you'll tune the gain once for a fixed spot. **The KY-038 is the trap** — it's what makes people say "my sound-reactive project barely works."

**Measuring loudness is easy; splitting frequencies is the finicky stretch.** The easy, reliable method needs no FFT at all: sample the mic over a **~50 ms window and take max − min** (peak-to-peak) — that's the loudness, straight from Adafruit's own tutorial ([Adafruit — Measuring Sound Levels](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-microphone-amplifier-breakout/measuring-sound-levels)). Beat detection (watch for loudness jumps) is a small step further and still easy. **Per-frequency is where a plain Arduino strains.** An Uno/Nano is a 16 MHz, 8-bit, 2 KB-RAM board; its ADC samples at only **~9.6 kHz by default** (usable audio to ~4–5 kHz), stretchable to ~76 kHz but only at **8–9 bits instead of 10** ([Zbotic — fast analog read](https://zbotic.in/arduino-fast-analog-read-increase-adc-speed-10x/), [USC EE459 ATmega328P ADC](https://ece-classes.usc.edu/ee459/library/documents/ADC.pdf)). The [**arduinoFFT** library](https://github.com/kosme/arduinoFFT/wiki) does run on an Uno, but RAM caps it at about **128 samples → 64 frequency bins**, and a benchmark clocks **~93 ms per 128-sample FFT (~10 Hz refresh)** ([Norwegian Creations — Arduino FFT pt.2](https://www.norwegiancreations.com/2019/03/arduino-fft-pt-2-improving-the-hardware-for-real-time-analysis/), [what-is-FFT-on-Arduino](https://www.norwegiancreations.com/2017/08/what-is-fft-and-how-can-you-implement-it-on-an-arduino/)). Translation: a **coarse** bass/mid/treble split is doable and fun, but a smooth, detailed spectrum is finicky — that's the honest reason the weekend build is "reacts to loudness," not "reacts to notes." (If the per-band show becomes the goal, that's the moment to consider an ESP32-class board, which has the speed and RAM for real-time FFT — a note for later, not the starter.)

**The LEDs draw more current than a USB port can give — this is the real ceiling.** Each **WS2812B LED draws up to ~60 mA at full-white** (red+green+blue all maxed) ([Adafruit NeoPixel Uberguide — powering](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-neopixel-uberguide/powering-neopixels)). So a USB 2.0 port's **500 mA runs ~8 LEDs at full white**; a 5 V / 2 A phone charger runs **~33**. A 16-LED ring is **~0.96 A at full white** (over a plain USB port's budget, fine on a 2 A charger); a 24-LED ring is **~1.44 A**. The saving grace: full-white-everywhere almost never happens in a music animation — Adafruit plans at **~20 mA/pixel** for real patterns ([same guide](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-neopixel-uberguide/powering-neopixels)), so a 16-LED ring pulsing colors typically pulls ~0.3 A and lives happily on USB. **The rule for this build:** keep the ring short (≤16 LEDs) and cap the brightness in code, and one USB port carries it. Grow past that and you need an external 5 V supply — **check this yourself** when you scale up (see Safety).

**Addressable LEDs have three wiring rules you skip at your peril.** From Adafruit's best-practices ([NeoPixel Uberguide — best practices](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-neopixel-uberguide/best-practices)): (1) put a **500–1000 µF capacitor (6.3 V+)** across + and − at the strip's input, so the inrush at power-on doesn't spike and kill the first LED; (2) put a **300–500 Ω resistor** in series on the data wire to protect that first pixel; (3) **share ground** — if the LEDs and the Arduino ever run off different supplies, their grounds *must* be joined. One more: WS2812B want a **5 V data signal**, so a 3.3 V board needs a **level shifter (74AHCT125)**, but a **5 V Arduino Uno/Nano drives the data line directly** — one reason a classic 5 V Arduino is the simpler starter here ([same guide](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-neopixel-uberguide/best-practices)).

**The printed diffuser is a real print, and the material choice matters.** A translucent shade printed **thin (≈1–2 mm wall)** in **vase mode** (one continuous single-wall spiral, no seam) is the standard way to turn a hard ring of LED dots into an even glow ([New Screwdriver — LED diffusion test](https://newscrewdriver.com/2019/07/18/glow-flow-led-diffusion-test-3d-printed-sheet/)). **Natural/white translucent filament diffuses better than clear** — clear passes more light but shows every LED as a hotspot, while a milky wall smooths them out ([Hackaday — clear PLA diffuses LEDs](https://hackaday.com/2023/02/20/clear-pla-diffuses-leds/)). A clean translucent print rewards a dialed-in printer: cross-link your own tuning work — [`guides/temperature-tower/`](../guides/temperature-tower/) for the temperature that gives smooth even walls and [`guides/retraction-vs-stringing/`](../guides/retraction-vs-stringing/) so the thin shade comes out clean, not hairy. **Heat honesty:** PLA's glass transition is only **~55–60 °C** vs PETG's **~80–85 °C** ([SigmaFilament PLA vs PETG](https://sigmafilament.com/pla-vs-petg-differences/)) — WS2812B run cool at the low brightness this lamp uses, so **PLA is fine**, but if you ever run the ring bright and close to the shade, **PETG is the safer, more heat-tolerant pick**.

## 6 · What does it need?

- **Microphone board:** a **MAX9814** (auto-gain, best for varying music) or **MAX4466** (manual gain). **Not** the KY-038 from the starter kit for the reactive version.
- **LEDs:** a short **WS2812B ring or strip** — a **16-LED ring** is the sweet spot for a desk lamp on USB.
- **Board:** an **Arduino Uno or Nano** (5 V, so it drives the LED data line with no level shifter).
- **The three-part LED wiring kit:** a **500–1000 µF capacitor (6.3 V+)** and a **300–500 Ω resistor**, plus common ground.
- **Power:** one **USB port / 5 V phone charger** for the short-ring version; an **external 5 V supply** only if you grow the LED count (Q4 math).
- **Libraries:** [Adafruit NeoPixel or FastLED](https://learn.adafruit.com/adafruit-neopixel-uberguide) for the LEDs; [arduinoFFT](https://github.com/kosme/arduinoFFT/wiki) *only* if you attempt the per-frequency stretch.
- **Filament + printer:** translucent/natural **PLA** (or **PETG** if bright/warm) for the diffuser, printed thin / vase mode.
- **Claude** to write and explain the sketch, the wiring, and the shade model. **You** solder the few joints, wire it, and slice/start/watch every print.

## 7 · Who does what — me, Claude, or both?

- **You:** buy the right mic (not the KY-038), wire it up (the cap, the resistor, the shared ground), and — as always — **slice, start, and watch every print** of the shade. You also flash the sketch to the Arduino.
- **Claude:** writes the amplitude-reactive sketch (sample window → loudness → brightness/color) with every value named in plain language; writes the wiring walkthrough with the cap/resistor/ground rules called out; designs the vase-mode diffuser model; and — per the teaching doctrine ([`docs/teaching-style.md`](../docs/teaching-style.md)) — builds an **animated HTML explainer** showing sound-wave → measured loudness → LED brightness, so the loop is something you can *watch*, not just read. Claude never sends G-code to the printer and never marks a print "safe to run unattended."
- **Both:** decide when (if ever) to reach for the per-frequency FFT stretch — and whether that's the cue to move to an ESP32-class board.
