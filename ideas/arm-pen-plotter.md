# Arm pen plotter — the arm draws wobbly line art (and that's the charm)

> **State:** grown via the ritual (2026-07-13) → verdict: **build** (start with a single straight line, not portraits)

The 6-servo arm holds a printed, spring-loaded pen and draws simple line art on paper, with Claude turning a drawing into a small, clamped, safe serial motion script — and the honest promise is *charming wobbly lines*, not printer-perfect ones.

*(Bench terms, one clause each: **inverse kinematics (IK)** = the math that turns a target point on the paper into the joint angles that reach it; **teach-mode / waypoints** = physically jogging the arm to key spots and recording the raw servo values to replay later, no math; **backlash** = the tiny slack in a gear train, so a joint lands a degree or two off when it reverses; **deadband** = a small band of commanded change a servo simply ignores, so it doesn't move until you push past it; **compliance / flexure** = a springy or gravity-loaded pen holder that gives a few mm so pen pressure stays even on an uneven surface; **clamp / envelope** = the safe angle range you measured per joint, plus the software that trims every command back inside it before it reaches a motor.)*

## The 8 questions

**1. What is this thing, really?**
It's the arm doing the same trick as a cheap pen plotter: hold a pen, move it across paper along a path, lift between strokes. The canonical version of exactly this — cheap microservos, wood, a pen — is [BrachioGraph](https://www.brachiograph.art/), which openly ships *"cheerful, low-fi drawings"* that Hackaday fondly calls [*"adorably wiggly"*](https://hackaday.com/2019/10/30/brachiograph-a-simple-and-cheap-pen-plotter/). That wiggle is the honest identity of this machine, not a defect to chase away.

**2. What could it grow into?**
A little drawing studio: single-stroke-font signatures and labels, generative line art, repeatable greeting cards, plotting a design before you commit it to a real cut. The software path (parse a drawing → points → clamped servo commands) is reusable for anything the arm should trace, and the calibration you build here is the same calibration every future arm motion needs.

**3. What's the coolest version of the simplest build?**
A single straight horizontal line, drawn cleanly and repeatably — then a square, then a circle. That is genuinely the coolest first win, because it *proves the whole pipeline* (calibration → clamp → serial → pen down → move → pen up) end to end. Portraits are explicitly not the first build: with this servo class the fine detail is below the machine's resolution and would come out as mush. Real builders start simple — the [Robertleoj two-joint arm](https://github.com/Robertleoj/pen_plotter_robot/blob/main/story.md)'s first real success was a single heart, and pen-plotter calibration guides universally begin with [squares and circles before any portrait work](https://github.com/laserpilot/Pen-Plotter-Calibration).

**4. What breaks it? (be honest)**
The dominant enemy is **servo slop, and it stacks**. BrachioGraph measures a [deadband of *"more than 15µs when changing directions"*](https://github.com/evildmp/BrachioGraph/blob/master/docs/explanation/hardware-limitations.rst) — across a servo's ~1000–2000µs range that's roughly **~1.5° of slop every time a joint reverses**, and drawing reverses constantly. On top of that a plastic-gear [SG90](https://servodatabase.com/servo/towerpro/sg90) adds ~1–2° of gear backlash; a metal-gear [MG996R](https://servodatabase.com/servo/towerpro/mg996r) helps durability but users still report poor repeatability and a long deadband. These errors are per-joint and they *stack along the arm* — a fraction of a degree at the shoulder becomes millimetres of wiggle at the pen tip, because a long lever multiplies small angular errors. Servos are also [*"far from being perfectly linear"* and show hysteresis even unloaded](https://github.com/evildmp/BrachioGraph/blob/master/docs/explanation/hardware-limitations.rst), so each one needs its own calibration curve. Two more real breakers: the **paper plane is never level** to the arm base, so a fixed Z digs in on one corner and floats off the opposite one; and **pen pressure** on that imperfect surface has to stay in a narrow window — too hard drags or stalls a servo, too light skips. The upshot: line quality is shaky, and *that shaky charm IS the aesthetic* — BrachioGraph proudly leans into ["adorably wiggly"](https://hackaday.com/2019/10/30/brachiograph-a-simple-and-cheap-pen-plotter/) output rather than fighting physics with more code.

**5. What does it let me build next?**
It compounds hard. The moment `arm/calibration.json` exists and every move is clamped, *any* arm project gets safer and easier — pick-and-place, teach-mode replays, camera-guided moves. The pen holder is a printed part you'll iterate, which feeds straight into the printed-end-effectors idea. And the SVG→points→motion pipeline is the general "make the arm follow a path" tool.

**6. What does it need?**
- **Parts:** the arm, a pen, and a printed **compliant pen holder** — a spring-loaded or gravity-loaded carriage so the pen self-adjusts to surface height ([springy pen holder STL](https://www.thingiverse.com/thing:1926664), [CNC Sharpie spring-loaded holder](https://www.printables.com/model/62825-cnc-sharpie-holder-spring-loaded)), optionally a small dedicated **pen-up/pen-down servo** ([pen lifter STL](https://www.printables.com/model/110358-pen-lifter-for-cnc-plotter)). The [Robertleoj build](https://github.com/Robertleoj/pen_plotter_robot/blob/main/story.md) shows the simplest option: *"the weight of the pen is enough downwards pressure,"* no spring at all.
- **This printed pen holder is the PRINTER↔ARM CROSSOVER** — the physical bridge between the two hobbies, and the single part most worth iterating on.
- **Power (non-negotiable):** servo power is a **separate, fused 5–6 V supply with shared ground and a reachable switch, sized for stall current — NEVER the Arduino's 5 V pin**. See the [envelope guide's safety rules](../guides/arm-envelope-explained/guide.md).
- **Software:** Python for parsing + serial; [ikpy](https://github.com/Phylliade/ikpy) later for IK you can validate in simulation on the laptop before any hardware moves; [numpy.polyfit](https://github.com/evildmp/BrachioGraph/blob/master/docs/explanation/hardware-limitations.rst) for the per-servo calibration curve; [svg2gcode](https://github.com/sameer/svg2gcode) and [vpype](https://purisa.me/blog/pen-plotter/) as references for the SVG→path idea (we won't emit literal G-code — we'll stream clamped servo commands).
- **The honest path, not hero IK:** the simplest beginner route is **teach-mode / recorded waypoints plus a polyfit calibration** — [BrachioGraph's approach](https://github.com/evildmp/BrachioGraph/blob/master/docs/explanation/hardware-limitations.rst), which bakes in your arm's real quirks instead of assuming ideal geometry. Graduate to [ikpy](https://github.com/Phylliade/ikpy) (validated in sim first) only once a straight line already works. Do NOT hand-derive 6-DOF IK on day one.
- **SVG→motion:** SVG → Inkscape [`Path > Object to Path`](https://wiki.opensourceecology.org/wiki/Using_Inkscape_to_Generate_Gcode) → parse points in Python → scale to the paper rectangle → run each point through calibration → **clamped** serial servo commands, pen-up between disconnected strokes.

**7. Who does what — me, Claude, or both?**
- **You:** measure each servo's safe min/max/center by hand and create `arm/calibration.json` FIRST (the [envelope guide](../guides/arm-envelope-explained/guide.md) walks every step), wire the **external fused servo supply**, print and fit the pen holder, tape down the paper, and — the hard safety rule — **watch every single powered move with a hand on the switch.**
- **Claude:** write the small Python scripts — the teach-mode waypoint recorder, the calibration-curve fitter, and the SVG→path→motion converter — with **every command routed through `clamp()` against your `arm/calibration.json`**, small and reviewable. No motion code merges without the clamp in the path.
- **Both:** compare the first wobbly line to what was commanded and tune the calibration together.

**8. What's the smallest piece I could finish this weekend?**
Two things, in order: (1) a filled-in `arm/calibration.json` from your own measured servo limits, and (2) one clamped teach-mode move that draws a **single straight line** with the pen holder. That's a finished, real, safe result by Sunday — the whole pipeline proven on the simplest possible shape. Square and circle are next weekend; a name in a single-stroke font is a great week-2 goal; portraits stay out of scope for this servo class.

## Verdict

**build.** This is a genuinely good weekend-scale beginner build, and the research backs the honest framing: an SG90/MG996R-class arm draws visibly shaky, wavy lines, so the goal is *charming wobbly line art*, not a precise printer — [BrachioGraph](https://www.brachiograph.art/), the canonical version of this exact project, proudly ships ["adorably wiggly"](https://hackaday.com/2019/10/30/brachiograph-a-simple-and-cheap-pen-plotter/) output and that character is the whole appeal. It's also the ideal **printer↔arm crossover**: the printed spring-loaded pen holder is the physical bridge, and the software (drawing → points → clamped serial commands) is small and safe. The build sequences calibration before any drawing motion, on purpose.

**First steps Claude will set up (calibration BEFORE any drawing code):**
1. **Create `arm/calibration.json` from your own measured servo min/max/center** — follow the [envelope guide](../guides/arm-envelope-explained/guide.md) (measure by hand, power off first, then slow powered sweeps). No motion code runs until this exists. This is *your* file, born from *your* measurements.
2. **A teach-mode waypoint recorder in Python that clamps every command** to `arm/calibration.json` — jog the arm, record raw servo values, replay them; every value routed through `clamp()`, and every powered move watched with a hand on the external, fused supply's switch.
3. **Print and fit the compliant pen holder** (spring-loaded or gravity) plus an optional pen-up/pen-down servo — the printer↔arm crossover part, iterated against the first test strokes.
4. **The single-line demo** — draw one straight horizontal line, then a square, then a circle, tuning the per-servo calibration curve ([polyfit](https://github.com/evildmp/BrachioGraph/blob/master/docs/explanation/hardware-limitations.rst)) as you go. Celebrate the wobble.
5. **SVG→path→motion converter** — SVG → Inkscape Object-to-Path → parse points in Python → scale to paper → clamped serial commands with pen-up between strokes; [ikpy](https://github.com/Phylliade/ikpy) validated in simulation only once a straight line already works.
