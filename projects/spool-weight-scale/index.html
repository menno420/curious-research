How a load cell + HX711 turn a spool's weight into "grams of filament left"

Set a spool on the platform and it tells you roughly how much filament is left. Watch the four things that have to happen in a row: the bar feels the weight, a chip turns that into a number, calibration turns the number into grams, and subtracting the empty spool gives grams remaining.

What you just watched

A spool's weight bends a little metal bar (the load cell) a hair; a strain gauge — a foil zig-zag on the bar — changes its resistance as it stretches, so the bar "feels" the weight. That change is microscopic, so the HX711 chip amplifies it and its ADC (analog-to-digital converter) turns it into a big raw number. That number isn't grams until you calibrate: once, you set a known weight on it and the sketch works out the counts-per-gram (the calibration factor) — after that, every raw count becomes real grams. Finally, the scale only ever reads the total weight, so filament left = total − the empty spool's own weight, which you weigh once and save. That's the whole chain: bend → number → grams → grams remaining.

What this animation exaggerates or leaves out

The bar's bend is hugely exaggerated — a real load cell deflects only microns, far too little to see. The raw numbers (843,217, 421,600, 843.2) are illustrative, not read off a real cell — yours will differ, which is exactly why you calibrate with your own known weight. And empty spools vary a lot — roughly 80–306 g depending on cardboard vs plastic and brand — so always use your own measured tare, never a guessed number.

Honest about what it is: this is an on-demand spot-check, not an always-on live display. A cheap load cell drifts a few grams with temperature and time, so re-zero it each time you use it and trust it for "do I have enough to start this print?" — not for milligram precision.
