Teach-mode, watched — jog, record a waypoint, replay through the clamp

This is how you draw your first line with the arm and no math: nudge the joints to a spot, save it, nudge to the next spot, save it — then replay the saved spots slowly. Every angle is trimmed to your measured safe range before it reaches a motor.

What you just watched

1. In teach-mode you don't do any geometry. You jog the arm — nudge one joint a few degrees at a time — until the pen is where you want it.

2. When the pen is on the spot, you record a waypoint: a snapshot of all six joint angles, saved to a plain file. Here we recorded two — the start and end of one line.

3. Ask a joint to go past its safe limit and the clamp — clamp(angle, min, max) — trims the number down to the edge before the motor moves. The joint stops at the safe edge; the pen never leaves the safe zone.

4. Replay eases the arm from waypoint to waypoint, slowly, drawing the line. Every replayed value passes through the clamp again. And it wobbles — that's the hobby-servo character, not a bug. Celebrate the wobble.

Safety, for real

  • No calibration file, no motion. The real tool refuses to run until you've measured your arm into arm/calibration.json. The clamp is only as safe as those measured numbers.
  • Servo power is external, always — a separate 5–6 V supply, shared ground, fused, reachable switch. Never the Arduino's 5 V pin.
  • A human watches every move, hand on the switch. Nothing is ever safe unattended.

What this animation simplifies

It shows a 2-joint arm from the side; yours has six servos moving in 3-D. The clamp really trims joint angles (shown here as the pen halting at the safe edge). And the wobble is drawn gently — a real hobby arm is often wobblier. Measure first, clamp always, watch every time.
